What is Bluexthermal’s (BXT) thin film, on the chip (OTC), evaporative cooling ?

BXT’s technology has a few defining characteristics over other types of 2 phase cooling. The Primary difference is that it is continuous meniscus. This means that unlike boiling where the bubbles form a meniscus which then grows and collapses, our technology allows for a much more stable and predictable evaporation mechanism. (see diagram). Before adding multiple blades into the mix, we also have a 3D component on every single blade in the form of the micro channels. This means that there is more overall surface area that can form a thin film region which is required for efficient 2 phase evaporation. This compares to boiling (or nucleated boiling) in which the total thin film surface area can’t exceed the area of the heat source. When the chip is set on top, the blades heat up and the many microchannels draw up the water like a sponge, setting the stage for the meniscus and the 2 phase evaporation to take place. The vapor escape (line) is through a side portal and can travel a great distance.

The second characteristic is the 3D structure formed by combining multiple blades stacked vertically along the face of the heat source. This has four benefits; (1) the first being additional active surface area for evaporation closer to the heat source, (2) the second is a dedicated vapor space to allow the vapor to freely leave the device and transport the collected heat away to whatever desired rejection system is desired in the application, (3) the third is a continuous liquid feed to the active site that has very high flow capacity per square area of heat source. This means the device can serve other applications where temperatures may be higher, but overall power density is above 10MW/m^2, where nearly every other 2 phase technology we are aware of fails due to dryout on any scale larger than ~1cmx1cm, and (4) the fourth benefit is that we can work with a bulk liquid pool. This allows a number of advantageous configurations such as immersion cooling, or alternatively in a closed loop configuration, and allows the heat to be carried much further from the source in a form that is higher quality heat to possibly be used similar to co-generation.

The BXT prototype testing used an Intel 10900 with the heat spreader removed against the copper surface above the blades (see prototype photo). The patent provides for it, and we believe the final endpoint of an “on the chip” set up may likely come in the form of implementing the technology directly into the silicon die of the chips. This would eliminate a number of critical thermal resistances which inhibit overall system performance. In a typical small/medium die size CPU situation, only ~⅓ of the temperature rise seen between the heat source and heat sink comes from the performance of the cooling technology. The other ⅔ come from the thermal interfaces and extra material required for structural integrity. With an etched on die approach, this could likely remove more than ½ of the currently unavailable losses. However, the difficulty of making a system work directly on the die is twofold. First, it needs to handle heat at a much higher power density as there will be no opportunity to spread the heat laterally (Taking heat load of a typical CPU from 1MW/m^2 to >2.5MW/m^2). This is not necessarily a problem in the heat range of today’s CPU’s. However, as the nodes continue to shrink, the power density has only grown, not to mention stacked chip architectures. The second challenge is that it needs to be low force and non-abrasive. Due to this, single phase approaches that are able to handle the necessary heat load are typically not suitable for integration directly into the silicon, which leaves them at a significant disadvantage vs. 2 phase,, having to bond to the silicon increasing thermal resistance.

What is it about thin film evaporative cooling that promises better chip cooling than traditional water cooling or immersion cooling ( boiling) ? Other advantages.

As a general principle, 2-phase cooling is much more powerful than single phase. Liquid water has a heat capacity of 4j/g*K, while evaporation carries away 2260j/g (at nearly 0 degrees delta T). This means that to get a similar cooling performance, you either need to pump 500x more liquid, or accept a much higher delta T between the heat source and the working fluid. Ultimately from the literature and experiments BXT conducted, this results in performance in the range of 100kW/m^2*K. With boiling, in an immersion cooling scenario we expect that to be much lower (note: have not been able to find reliable sources for current state of the art for immersion boiling). Without fully optimizing, we have simulated versions of our prototype/structure that should yield performance between 250-300kW/m^2*K. This performance would also be expected to hold up even in an immersion setup, by simply latching on the BXT device. We believe that this step alone would yield significant benefits for most existing immersion technologies.

Bluexthermal succeeded in building and testing a prototype of its technology which was compared in a side by side test with an established water block cooling product. What do those results suggest to us ?

The BXT prototype was able to confirm a few things, but also highlighted a number of steps that need to be completed to achieve full theoretical performance. We were able to construct a full closed loop system that had a performance on par with today’s highest performance off the shelf waterblocks, but operated on the principles of 3D continuous meniscus thin film evaporation.

This took the technology a step beyond the work performed at the University of Virginia (UVA) which was mostly done in an open system with materials, heat sources, and working fluids designed only to prove the underlying physics instead of maximizing heat removal in a real world environment. These experiments performed at BXT were able to show that in a relatively similar form-factor to existing technologies, we could construct a working system that did not show any fundamental pitfalls. During this round of

experimentation however, we were not able to achieve performance levels as high as what is predicted by the theoretical models. We believe the number one contributor to this was the lack of a robust super hydrophilic coating that is needed to achieve the theoretical thin film geometry. In the UVA experiments this was not an issue due to the working fluid and substrate materials involved, which naturally form a near 0 degree contact angle, but are not necessarily ideal for a real world heat rejection system. Between the two sets of experiments performed between UVA and BXT, we believe that we proved the theoretical basis for the design, as well as confirmed that any remaining work to get to a full performance real world model is merely an engineering challenge rather than an exercise of pure science.
